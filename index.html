Statics

Equilibrium Equations

Statics
∑F = 0, ∑M = 0
ε Strain (dimensionless)
ΔL Change in length (m)
L₀ Original length (m)

Stress is the internal resistance of a material to deformation, calculated as force per unit area. Strain is the measure of deformation, calculated as the change in length divided by the original length.

Hooke's Law

Materials
σ = E·ε, τ = G·γ
σ Normal stress (Pa)
E Young's modulus (Pa)
ε Normal strain
τ Shear stress (Pa)
G Shear modulus (Pa)
γ Shear strain

Hooke's Law states that, for small deformations, the strain in a material is proportional to the applied stress. The constant of proportionality is the elastic modulus (E for normal stress/strain, G for shear).

Poisson's Ratio

Materials
ν = -εᵗᵣₐₙₛ/εₗₒₙₐ
ν Poisson's ratio
εᵗᵣₐₙₛ Transverse strain
εₗₒₙₐ Longitudinal strain

Poisson's ratio is the negative ratio of transverse strain to longitudinal strain. It characterizes how a material deforms perpendicular to the direction of loading. For most materials, the value is between 0 and 0.5.

Heat Transfer

Fourier's Law of Conduction

Heat Transfer
q = -k·A·(dT/dx)
q Heat transfer rate (W)
k Thermal conductivity (W/(m·K))
A Cross-sectional area (m²)
dT/dx Temperature gradient (K/m)

Fourier's Law describes heat transfer by conduction, stating that the heat transfer rate is proportional to the negative temperature gradient and the area through which the heat flows.

Newton's Law of Cooling

Heat Transfer
q = h·A·(Ts - T∞)
q Heat transfer rate (W)
h Heat transfer coefficient (W/(m²·K))
A Surface area (m²)
Ts Surface temperature (K)
T∞ Fluid temperature (K)

Newton's Law of Cooling describes heat transfer by convection, stating that the rate of heat loss is proportional to the difference in temperature between the object and its surroundings.

Stefan-Boltzmann Law

Heat Transfer
q = ε·σ·A·(T₁⁴ - T₂⁴)
q Heat transfer rate (W)
ε Emissivity
σ Stefan-Boltzmann constant (5.67×10⁻⁸ W/(m²·K⁴))
A Surface area (m²)
T₁, T₂ Absolute temperatures (K)

The Stefan-Boltzmann Law describes heat transfer by radiation, stating that the energy radiated by a black body is proportional to the fourth power of its absolute temperature.

∑F Sum of all forces
∑M Sum of all moments

For a body to be in static equilibrium, the sum of all forces and moments acting on it must equal zero. This is the foundation of statics problems.

Moment of Force

Statics
M = r × F = r·F·sin(θ)
M Moment (N·m)
r Position vector (m)
F Force (N)
θ Angle between r and F

The moment of a force is a measure of its tendency to cause rotation about a point or axis, equal to the product of the force and the perpendicular distance to the point or axis.

Centroid of Common Shapes

Statics
Rectangle: x̄ = L/2, ȳ = H/2
x̄, ȳ Centroid coordinates
L Length
H Height

The centroid is the geometric center of a shape. For a rectangular shape, it is located at half the length and half the height from the origin.

Dynamics

Newton's Second Law

Dynamics
F = m·a
F Force (N)
m Mass (kg)
a Acceleration (m/s²)

Newton's Second Law states that the acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

Kinetic Energy

Dynamics
KE = ½·m·v²
KE Kinetic energy (J)
m Mass (kg)
v Velocity (m/s)

Kinetic energy is the energy possessed by an object due to its motion. It is directly proportional to the mass and the square of the velocity.

Angular Motion

Dynamics
ω = θ/t, α = ω/t, τ = I·α
ω Angular velocity (rad/s)
θ Angular displacement (rad)
α Angular acceleration (rad/s²)
τ Torque (N·m)
I Moment of inertia (kg·m²)

These equations describe angular motion. Angular velocity is the rate of change of angular position, angular acceleration is the rate of change of angular velocity, and torque is proportional to angular acceleration.

Thermodynamics

First Law of Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics
ΔU = Q - W
ΔU Change in internal energy (J)
Q Heat added to system (J)
W Work done by system (J)

The First Law of Thermodynamics states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed. The change in internal energy of a system equals the heat added minus the work done by the system.

Thermal Efficiency

Thermodynamics
η = W/Qin = 1 - Qout/Qin = 1 - Tc/Th
η Thermal efficiency
W Work output (J)
Qin Heat input (J)
Qout Heat rejected (J)
Tc Cold reservoir temperature (K)
Th Hot reservoir temperature (K)

Thermal efficiency is the ratio of work output to heat input in a heat engine. The last formula represents the Carnot efficiency, which is the maximum possible efficiency for a heat engine operating between two temperatures.

Ideal Gas Law

Thermodynamics
PV = nRT
P Pressure (Pa)
V Volume (m³)
n Number of moles
R Gas constant (8.314 J/(mol·K))
T Temperature (K)

The Ideal Gas Law relates pressure, volume, temperature, and the number of moles of a gas. It is an equation of state that approximates the behavior of many gases under many conditions.

Fluid Mechanics

Bernoulli's Equation

Fluid Mechanics
P₁ + ½ρv₁² + ρgh₁ = P₂ + ½ρv₂² + ρgh₂
P Pressure (Pa)
ρ Fluid density (kg/m³)
v Fluid velocity (m/s)
g Gravitational acceleration (m/s²)
h Height (m)

Bernoulli's equation relates pressure, velocity, and elevation in a moving fluid. It is derived from the principle of conservation of energy and is valid for incompressible, inviscid flow along a streamline.

Continuity Equation

Fluid Mechanics
A₁v₁ = A₂v₂
A Cross-sectional area (m²)
v Fluid velocity (m/s)

The continuity equation expresses the conservation of mass in fluid flow. For incompressible flow, it states that the product of the cross-sectional area and velocity remains constant along a streamline.

Reynolds Number

Fluid Mechanics
Re = ρvL/μ
Re Reynolds number (dimensionless)
ρ Fluid density (kg/m³)
v Fluid velocity (m/s)
L Characteristic length (m)
μ Dynamic viscosity (kg/(m·s))

The Reynolds number is a dimensionless quantity that predicts fluid flow patterns. It represents the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces. Low values indicate laminar flow, while high values indicate turbulent flow.
