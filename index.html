Statics

Equilibrium Equations

Statics
∑F = 0, ∑M = 0
∑F Sum of all forces
∑M Sum of all moments

For a body to be in static equilibrium, the sum of all forces and moments acting on it must equal zero. This is the foundation of statics problems.

Moment of Force

Statics
M = r × F = r·F·sin(θ)
M Moment (N·m)
r Position vector (m)
F Force (N)
θ Angle between r and F

The moment of a force is a measure of its tendency to cause rotation about a point or axis, equal to the product of the force and the perpendicular distance to the point or axis.

Centroid of Common Shapes

Statics
Rectangle: x̄ = L/2, ȳ = H/2
x̄, ȳ Centroid coordinates
L Length
H Height

The centroid is the geometric center of a shape. For a rectangular shape, it is located at half the length and half the height from the origin.

Dynamics

Newton's Second Law

Dynamics
F = m·a
F Force (N)
m Mass (kg)
a Acceleration (m/s²)

Newton's Second Law states that the acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

Kinetic Energy

Dynamics
KE = ½·m·v²
KE Kinetic energy (J)
m Mass (kg)
v Velocity (m/s)

Kinetic energy is the energy possessed by an object due to its motion. It is directly proportional to the mass and the square of the velocity.

Angular Motion

Dynamics
ω = θ/t, α = ω/t, τ = I·α
ω Angular velocity (rad/s)
θ Angular displacement (rad)
α Angular acceleration (rad/s²)
τ Torque (N·m)
I Moment of inertia (kg·m²)

These equations describe angular motion. Angular velocity is the rate of change of angular position, angular acceleration is the rate of change of angular velocity, and torque is proportional to angular acceleration.

Thermodynamics

First Law of Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics
ΔU = Q - W
ΔU Change in internal energy (J)
Q Heat added to system (J)
W Work done by system (J)

The First Law of Thermodynamics states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed. The change in internal energy of a system equals the heat added minus the work done by the system.

Thermal Efficiency

Thermodynamics
η = W/Qin = 1 - Qout/Qin = 1 - Tc/Th
η Thermal efficiency
W Work output (J)
Qin Heat input (J)
Qout Heat rejected (J)
Tc Cold reservoir temperature (K)
Th Hot reservoir temperature (K)

Thermal efficiency is the ratio of work output to heat input in a heat engine. The last formula represents the Carnot efficiency, which is the maximum possible efficiency for a heat engine operating between two temperatures.
